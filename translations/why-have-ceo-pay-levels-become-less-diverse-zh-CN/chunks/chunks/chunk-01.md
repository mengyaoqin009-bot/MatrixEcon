Abstract
This paper documents a new stylized fact; the cross-sectional variation in CEO pay lev-
els has declined precipitously in recent years. We offer one explanation for this decline:
firms are increasingly benchmarking CEO compensation to industry peers closest in
size, thereby creating pay clusters. Our empirical tests provide support for this ex-
planation and suggest that the rise of industry-size benchmarking is driven by the
combination of three institutional factors: the mandatory disclosure of compensation
peer groups, proxy advisory influence, and Say on Pay regulation. Our findings high-
light a consequence of adopting a one-size-fits-all standard in the pay setting process.
(JEL: G3, G34, G38, M12, M52)
Keywords: Variation in executive pay; pay dispersion; compensation benchmark-
ing; industry-size benchmarking; pay transparency; pay disclosure;
proxy advisers; say on pay

1 . Introduction
This paper documents a new stylized fact; over the last decade, the cross-sectional variation
in CEO pay levels has declined precipitously (see Figure 1). Using a wide sample of publicly
listed U.S. firms, spanning from 1996 to 2023, we document a decline of almost 40 percent
in the cross-sectional variation in CEO pay from 2007 until recent times. The decline in
cross-sectional variation in CEO pay levels (henceforth “pay variation” or “pay dispersion”)
is due to convergence in pay towards the median in both tails of the pay distribution and
holds not only at the economy level but also within industries and industry-size groups.
The pattern is empirically robust. The downward trend is also observable in demand-
ing regression analyses using different degrees of data granularity (industry-size-year and
firm-year) to control for potential confounding effects. The lower pay dispersion is neither
due to a decline in the use of stock options nor due to an increase in the similarity of firm
fundamentals; we find the same pattern when we exclude from total compensation the value
of granted stock options and the portion of compensation associated with the main economic
determinants of CEO pay (i.e., dispersion in “abnormal pay”).
We offer one explanation for this secular decrease in pay variation: the rise of industry-
size benchmarking caused by the increased scrutiny of CEO pay by regulators, shareholders,
and proxy advisers. That is, compared to previous decades, firms are increasingly bench-
marking CEO compensation to the industry peers closest in size. The adoption of such a
uniform criterion across the economy can result in lower pay variation across firms to the
extent that the policy generates “clusters” of compensation peers in which firms benchmark
their pay to one another. As firms typically target pay at the median of their peers’ pay, the
practice of pay targeting inevitably reduces pay variation within such clusters which, as we
show below, can also reduce economy-wide pay dispersion.
We examine the factors driving the growth of industry-size benchmarking and find that
it is largely driven by three recent institutional developments: the 2006 SEC rule mandating
the disclosure of peer groups used for compensation benchmarking, the growing influence of
proxy advisors that favor industry-size benchmarks for CEO pay, and the introduction of

say on pay regulation. The confluence of these institutional developments – which enabled
greater scrutiny of firms’ pay practices by shareholders and proxy advisors – increased pres-
sure on boards to conform to a standardized pay-setting process. This, in turn, led to the
adoption of uniform criteria such as industry-size benchmarking.
Our findings highlight the implications of applying a uniform criterion – such as industry-
size benchmarking – in the executive pay-setting process. On the one hand, increased trans-
parency, coupled with standardized criteria, may facilitate the evaluation of pay practices
and promote greater accountability. Moreover, uniformity in pay-setting may enhance per-
ceptions of fairness in CEO compensation design, a key concern for directors and investors
(Edmans, Gosling and Jenter, 2023). On the other hand, institutional pressure from regula-
tors and investors to make CEO pay more transparent and standardized may lead boards to
prioritize compliance over customization. As a result, firms may adopt a “one-size-fits-all”
approach that sacrifices alignment with firm- and executive-specific needs, potentially lead-
ing to suboptimal incentive structures and inefficiencies. By documenting how adherence to
standardized benchmarks contributes to a decline in cross-sectional pay variation, our results
underscore the importance of better understanding how institutional and market pressures
influence CEO pay – both in terms of overall pay levels and dispersion.
To examine the empirical validity of our proposed explanation for the documented decline
in pay dispersion – namely, the widespread adoption of a uniform peer selection criterion
based on industry affiliation and firm size – we conduct three sets of analyses. As a first step,
we simulate the evolution of pay dispersion under the assumption that all firms adopt an
industry-size-based benchmarking approach. We find that simulated pay dispersion declines
substantially, lending support to the notion that industry-size benchmarking contributes to
the observed reduction in pay dispersion. This simulation is informative because the effect of
clustered compensation networks on economy-wide pay dispersion is not ex ante obvious: it
depends on the number and size of benchmarking groups (e.g., industry-size groups), changes
in the cross-sectional distribution of firm size and industry affiliation (which can be driven
by entry, exit, growth, or other dynamics), and residual cross-group connections through

In our second set of tests, we use regression analyses to examine whether the decrease in
pay dispersion goes hand in hand with the increasing tendency to benchmark CEO compen-
sation to the industry peers closest in size. The results reveal that, over time, compensation
peer groups exhibit less dispersion in size, are less likely to deviate from the industry-size cri-
terion, and show greater compliance with the industry-size based peer selection methodology
adopted by ISS (i.e., the largest proxy advisory firm). Critically, these patterns are strongly
associated with our results on pay dispersion both in the time-series and the cross-section.
In a third set of tests, we provide plausibly causal evidence on how three recent institu-
tional developments – namely the 2006 SEC rule that mandated the disclosure of the peers
used for compensation benchmarking, the influence of proxy advisors, and the introduction
of say on pay regulation – have jointly contributed to the rise of industry-size benchmarking.
Regarding the 2006 SEC pay disclosure mandate, we find that the decrease in pay disper-
sion starts after the implementation of the disclosure mandate that increased transparency
about the pay setting process, not before. Also consistent with the disclosure mandate play-
ing a role in the documented patterns, we observe that the increasing tendency to adopt
industry-size benchmarking is stronger among firms whose compensation peer groups ex-
hibit larger deviations from this criterion right before the introduction of the regulation
(which was relatively unexpected). In a series of falsification tests, we do not find a decline
in pay dispersion based on settings in which the 2006 SEC disclosure mandate plays a less
prominent role; among non-US public firms, among private U.S. firms, and among senior
executives outside the top 5 in public U.S. firms (whose pay is typically not determined via
compensation benchmarking).
Regarding the role of proxy advisers, we document that a firm’s probability of including
a potential peer critically varies with the degree of conformity the peer has with ISS’s policy
for compensation benchmarking, which we measure using peer rankings produced by ISS’s
publicly disclosed methodology. But perhaps more importantly in terms of explaining the
documented increase in industry-size benchmarking, we find that this association strength-

Regarding the influence of Say on Pay (SOP) regulation, we find that, following weak

industry-size benchmarking criterion.1 To sharpen identification, we exploit exogenous vari-

We compare firms whose shareholders voted for a 1-year SOP vote frequency to those whose
shareholders voted for a 3-year SOP vote frequency by a narrow margin in their first manda-
tory shareholder vote on the frequency of SOP voting. We find that firms whose shareholders
narrowly voted for a 1-year frequency exhibit greater conformity in the years after the vote
(but not before) with the industry-size benchmarking criterion than those in which the vot-
ing outcome was narrowly in favor of a 3-year frequency. These results support a causal role
for SOP advisory votes in encouraging the adoption of industry-size benchmarking.

sectional distribution of CEO pay levels. In contrast to the secular increase in the mean of
the cross-sectional distribution of CEO pay, we document a stark decline in the variation of
this distribution in recent years. We propose one driver of the documented decrease in pay
dispersion – industry-size benchmarking – but there could be others.
Two notable recent contributions to this literature are Cabezon (2025) and Kalpathy,
Nanda and Zhao (2024). Cabezon (2025) documents that the structure of compensation
contracts has become more similar across firms since 2006, with companies relying on more
similar compensation vehicles such as a similar fraction of pay in equity. However, greater
similarity in the compensation vehicles does not imply less dispersion in pay levels. For
example, firms could increase total compensation to offset utility losses associated with di-
minished flexibility in contract tailoring. Moreover, Cabezon’s analysis is not concerned with
compensation peer groups but with the average similarity between a focal firm’s contract
and the contracts of all other firms in the economy. In contrast, our study investigates
the rise of industry-size benchmarking and the institutional forces behind its growing preva-
lence. Kalpathy, Nanda and Zhao (2024) provide complementary evidence that lower-profile,
agency-prone firms tend to herd toward “star” peers in peer group selection to avoid share-
holder scrutiny. While their focus is on opportunistic peer selection, our analysis highlights
broader structural changes in peer benchmarking, emphasizing the influence of standardized
criteria and the institutional pressures driving their adoption.
We also contribute to the broader discussion on the costs and benefits of increased trans-
parency and scrutiny in corporate pay-setting processes (Mas, 2016; Baker et al., 2019;
Bennedsen et al., 2022; Babenko, Bennett and Wang, 2025). Specifically, we show how
regulations designed to enhance shareholder oversight can encourage the adoption of one-
size-fits-all compensation practices, thereby highlighting a potential unintended consequence
of well-intentioned governance reforms.
