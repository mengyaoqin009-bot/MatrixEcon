Abstract

> **中文：** 摘要

This paper documents a new stylized fact: the cross-sectional variation in CEO pay levels has declined precipitously in recent years. We offer one explanation for this decline: firms are increasingly benchmarking CEO compensation to industry peers closest in size, thereby creating pay clusters. Our empirical tests provide support for this explanation and suggest that the rise of industry-size benchmarking is driven by the combination of three institutional factors: the mandatory disclosure of compensation peer groups, proxy advisory influence, and Say on Pay regulation. Our findings highlight a consequence of adopting a one-size-fits-all standard in the pay-setting process.  
(JEL: G3, G34, G38, M12, M52)

1 . Introduction

> **中文：** 1．引言

This paper documents a new stylized fact: over the last decade, the cross-sectional variation in CEO pay levels has declined precipitously (see Figure 1). Using a wide sample of publicly listed U.S. firms, spanning from 1996 to 2023, we document a decline of almost 40 percent in the cross-sectional variation in CEO pay from 2007 until recent times. The decline in cross-sectional variation in CEO pay levels (henceforth “pay variation” or “pay dispersion”) is due to convergence in pay towards the median in both tails of the pay distribution and holds not only at the economy level but also within industries and industry-size groups. The pattern is empirically robust. The downward trend is also observable in demanding regression analyses using different degrees of data granularity (industry-size-year and firm-year) to control for potential confounding effects. The lower pay dispersion is neither due to a decline in the use of stock options nor due to an increase in the similarity of firm fundamentals; we find the same pattern when we exclude from total compensation the value of granted stock options and the portion of compensation associated with the main economic determinants of CEO pay (i.e., dispersion in “abnormal pay”).

> **中文：** 本文记录了一个新的典型事实：在过去十年间，CEO 薪酬水平的横截面差异急剧下降（见图 1）。我们使用涵盖 1996 年至 2023 年、范围广泛的美国上市公司样本，发现从 2007 年至近期，CEO 薪酬的横截面差异下降了近 40%。CEO 薪酬水平横截面差异的下降（下文称“薪酬差异”或“薪酬离散度”）源于薪酬分布两端均向中位数收敛；这一现象不仅存在于整体经济层面，也存在于行业内部和行业-规模组内部。这一模式在实证上具有稳健性。在使用不同数据粒度（行业-规模-年度和公司-年度）控制潜在混杂效应的严格回归分析中，也可以观察到这一下降趋势。薪酬离散度降低既不是因为股票期权使用减少，也不是因为公司基本面的相似程度提高；当我们从总薪酬中剔除授予股票期权的价值，以及与 CEO 薪酬主要经济决定因素相关的薪酬部分（即考察“异常薪酬”的离散度）时，仍然发现相同模式。

We offer one explanation for this secular decrease in pay variation: the rise of industry-size benchmarking caused by the increased scrutiny of CEO pay by regulators, shareholders, and proxy advisers. That is, compared to previous decades, firms are increasingly benchmarking CEO compensation to the industry peers closest in size. The adoption of such a uniform criterion across the economy can result in lower pay variation across firms to the extent that the policy generates “clusters” of compensation peers in which firms benchmark their pay to one another. As firms typically target pay at the median of their peers’ pay, the practice of pay targeting inevitably reduces pay variation within such clusters which, as we show below, can also reduce economy-wide pay dispersion.

> **中文：** 我们为薪酬差异的这一长期下降提供了一种解释：监管机构、股东和代理投票顾问对 CEO 薪酬的审查日益加强，推动了行业-规模基准比较的兴起。换言之，与此前数十年相比，公司越来越多地以规模最接近的行业同业公司作为 CEO 薪酬基准。在整个经济中采用这种统一标准可能降低公司之间的薪酬差异，因为该政策会形成若干薪酬同业公司的“集群”，集群内的公司相互参照来确定薪酬。由于公司通常将薪酬目标设定在同业公司薪酬的中位数，薪酬目标设定的做法必然会降低此类集群内部的薪酬差异；正如下文所示，这也可能降低整体经济范围内的薪酬离散度。

Our findings highlight the implications of applying a uniform criterion—such as industry-size benchmarking—in the executive pay-setting process. On the one hand, increased transparency, coupled with standardized criteria, may facilitate the evaluation of pay practices and promote greater accountability. Moreover, uniformity in pay-setting may enhance perceptions of fairness in CEO compensation design, a key concern for directors and investors (Edmans, Gosling and Jenter, 2023). On the other hand, institutional pressure from regulators and investors to make CEO pay more transparent and standardized may lead boards to prioritize compliance over customization. As a result, firms may adopt a “one-size-fits-all” approach that sacrifices alignment with firm- and executive-specific needs, potentially leading to suboptimal incentive structures and inefficiencies. By documenting how adherence to standardized benchmarks contributes to a decline in cross-sectional pay variation, our results underscore the importance of better understanding how institutional and market pressures influence CEO pay—both in terms of overall pay levels and dispersion.

In our second set of tests, we use regression analyses to examine whether the decrease in pay dispersion goes hand in hand with the increasing tendency to benchmark CEO compensation to the industry peers closest in size. The results reveal that, over time, compensation peer groups exhibit less dispersion in size, are less likely to deviate from the industry-size criterion, and show greater compliance with the industry-size based peer selection methodology adopted by ISS (i.e., the largest proxy advisory firm). Critically, these patterns are strongly associated with our results on pay dispersion both in the time-series and the cross-section.

In a third set of tests, we provide plausibly causal evidence on how three recent institutional developments—namely the 2006 SEC rule that mandated the disclosure of the peers used for compensation benchmarking, the influence of proxy advisors, and the introduction of say on pay regulation—have jointly contributed to the rise of industry-size benchmarking.
